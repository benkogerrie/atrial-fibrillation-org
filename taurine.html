Taurine and Atrial Fibrillation

Mechanisms, evidence and practical considerations

Overview

Taurine is a sulfurâ€‘containing amino acid present in high concentrations in the heart. It participates in calcium handling, osmoregulation, membrane stabilization, and modulation of autonomic tone. In the context of atrial fibrillation (AFib), taurine has attracted interest because it may stabilize cardiac electrophysiology and mitigate atrial remodeling. While human randomized AF trials are lacking, preclinical studies and case observations provide biologically plausible signals. This page summarizes mechanisms, evidence, dosage considerations, and safety. Use these insights to have an informed discussion with your cardiology team.

How Taurine Might Help in AFib

1) Electrophysiology and ion homeostasis

AFib is sustained by abnormal conduction and refractoriness. Taurine modulates intracellular Ca2+ handling and supports potassium balance, which can dampen afterdepolarizations and reduce ectopy. Animal models show shorter AF duration and normalization of channel expression when taurine is provided.

2) Structural remodeling

Chronic AF is maintained by structural changes including fibrosis, inflammation, and atrial dilation. In rat AF models, taurine reduced fibrosis and inflammatory signaling and limited dilationâ€”changes that typically make AF more persistent. Protecting the atrial substrate is a key preventive strategy alongside rhythm control.

3) Autonomic balance

Sympathetic predominance and vagal triggers both play roles in AF. Practical implications include pairing taurine with sleep, stress, and lifestyle optimization (see Triggers).

Safety and Interactions

Taurine is generally well tolerated. Potential adverse effects include GI discomfort at higher intakes. Interactions are uncommon but theoretically possible with certain antihypertensives or diuretics (electrolyte balance). Patients with kidney disease, pregnancy, or complex medication regimens should obtain individualized advice.
